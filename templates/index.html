Wealth, Education, and Equity Across Countries

From 1995 to 2018, national wealth does not simply signal economic capacity, it also reveals how far that capacity is converted into schooling, participation, and more equitable educational development.

I have built this interactive analysis to follow that relationship across countries and years, tracing where wealth level matters most, where wealth structure changes the story, and where inequality weakens the link between national resources and educational progress.

Data Sources Used

From World Bank source tables to analysis-ready views

I built this web app from the coursework data pipeline. The source foundation is the World Bank Changing Wealth of Nations (CWON) dataset plus selected World Development Indicators (WDI), cleaned and merged before the final Python notebook produced the analysis tables used here.

1. CWON

Source: World Bank Data360, Changing Wealth of Nations. This is the long-run wealth stock dataset behind the project's core explanatory variables.

Key variables used: wealth_pc, human_capital_pc, produced_capital_pc, natural_capital_pc, and net_foreign_assets_pc.

2. WDI

Source: World Bank World Development Indicators. These files provide the education, population, spending, inequality, and classification variables that let the wealth data be interpreted.

Indicators used here include education outcomes, population, GDP per capita, education spending, Gini, region, and income-group classifications.

3. Processing Flow

The source tables were cleaned in Power BI, merged into q_analysis_ready, exported as CSV, processed in the Python notebook, and then summarised into the final tables used by this app.

The final views used in the app are q_analysis_core, q_year_summary, q_year_region_summary, q_country_summary, q_q4_spend, and q_q4_gini.

4. How To Read The App

Research questions 1 and 2 mainly use country-year observations, research question 3 uses country averages, and the final question uses smaller matched subsets where spending or inequality data are available.

The visual narrative is therefore organised around country-year relationships, country-average performance, and smaller policy-focused subsets.

Research Question 1 How has the relationship between national wealth per capita and educational development changed over time? Wealth, schooling, and tertiary participation are presented as linked outcomes rather than separate stories.
Research Question 2 Does wealth composition matter as much as wealth level? Human, natural, and produced capital are framed as structural features of wealth rather than background context.
Research Question 3 Which countries overperform or underperform educationally relative to their wealth level? This section focuses on countries above and below the fitted wealth trend, not only on absolute averages.
Research Question 4 Among countries with similar wealth levels, do spending and inequality still matter? The final section frames education through investment, access, and inequality rather than wealth alone.

Reading The Full Picture

What the evidence points toward

Across the full dataset, wealth per capita remains strongly associated with educational development, but level alone does not tell the whole story. The visuals suggest that human and produced capital align more closely with stronger outcomes than natural-capital dependence, several countries perform meaningfully above or below their fitted wealth benchmark, and inequality remains the clearer policy signal once wealth is taken into account. Taken together, the evidence points toward long-horizon human-capital investment, stronger conversion of national wealth into educational capacity, and closer attention to equitable access rather than spending volume alone.
